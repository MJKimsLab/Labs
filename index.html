Gas barrier Graphene by blocking pores.

  The research team of Professor Myung-Jong Kim of the Department of Chemistry, together with the research team of Dr. Seok-Hoon Ahn of the Korea Institute of Science and Technology and the team of Professor Young-Jong Kang of Hanyang University, developed a technology to fill the nanopores while maintaining the unique characteristics of graphene by using the phenomenon of organic matter self-aligning on the graphene surface.

  Professor Kim's team uses the phenomenon that organic materials with long chain structures align well with each other at the molecular level on the surface of graphene to cover the nanopores of graphene with aligned organic molecules, thereby creating nanopores that become passageways for gas molecules. blocked By controlling the thickness of the aligned organic material at the nanometer level, it was possible to significantly improve the gas barrier properties by lowering the moisture permeability to 1/100 while maintaining the transparency and flexibility inherent in graphene.

A gacile method to synthesize white graphene

  Dr. Myung-Jong Kim's team at KIST obtained high-crystalline white graphene (h-BN) by dropping a polymer (borazine oligomer), a raw material, onto a nickel catalyst substrate, rotating it at high speed to spread it thinly, and then heat-treating it at 1000°C.

  According to the research team, the characteristics of this method are very simple compared to the conventional chemical vapor deposition (CVD) method using borazine as a raw material, and it is possible only with a low-cost device.

  Dr. Kim Myung-jong said, "The core of this study is to find out the mechanism of securing white graphene with high integrity at a relatively low temperature by the effect of the catalyst." As a result of application, it was found that the effect of reducing the doping effect of the graphene material and increasing the charge mobility related to the operating speed of the transistor by about two times was found.”
